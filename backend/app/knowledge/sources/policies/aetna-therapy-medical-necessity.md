# Aetna Clinical Policy — Medical Necessity: Outpatient Physical and Occupational Therapy

**Policy Number:** AET-THER-033
**Payer:** Aetna
**Effective:** January 1, 2026 | **Last reviewed:** February 2026
**Applies to:** Commercial HMO/PPO plans

## Policy statement

Outpatient physical therapy (CPT 97110, 97112, 97140, 97530 and related
codes) is considered medically necessary when the member has a documented
functional deficit that is expected to improve measurably with skilled
therapy, and the services require the skills of a licensed therapist.
Services that do not meet these criteria are denied with **CARC 50** (not
deemed a medical necessity), typically with remark code **N115** indicating
the decision was based on a coverage policy.

## Visit thresholds

An initial course of up to **twenty (20) visits per condition per benefit
year** is covered without clinical review when billed with a supporting
diagnosis. Visits beyond the threshold require submission of a continued
care request demonstrating:

1. Objective, measurable progress against the initial evaluation (range of
   motion, strength grades, standardized functional outcome scores);
2. An updated plan of care signed by the referring provider within the
   last ninety (90) days; and
3. A discharge plan including a transition to an independent home exercise
   program.

Claims exceeding the threshold without an approved continued care request
are denied under this policy even when individual visits were appropriate.

## Services not considered medically necessary

- Maintenance therapy where no functional improvement is expected or
  documented across three (3) consecutive visits;
- Duplicative therapy (physical and occupational therapy targeting the
  same deficit in the same period without distinct goals);
- Group exercise, fitness, or wellness programs;
- Passive modalities (hot/cold packs, unattended electrical stimulation)
  billed as the sole intervention beyond the first two weeks of care.

## Appeals

Medical necessity denials under this policy are **appealable**. A
first-level appeal must be filed within 180 calendar days of the remittance
date and should include the initial evaluation, daily treatment notes for
the denied dates, objective progress measurements, and the signed plan of
care. Appeals that map the clinical record explicitly to the criteria in
this policy are resolved substantially faster than record dumps; Aetna's
internal data show an overturn rate above sixty percent when objective
progress measures are included.

Do not resubmit the claim unchanged: an unchanged resubmission of a CARC 50
denial will auto-deny as a duplicate. The appeal pathway is the only route
to payment for services already rendered.
